## History of the Committee

Established in 1945 as one of the six principal organs of the United Nations, the Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC) serves as the primary body for coordination, policy review, and recommendations on economic, social, and environmental issues.[^10] While the Security Council focuses on immediately stopping conflicts, ECOSOC addresses the root causes of global instability by fostering international cooperation to improve standards of living and promote sustainable development. Today, the Council consists of 54 Member States elected by the General Assembly for overlapping three-year terms, ensuring broad geographical representation of the global community.[^15]

Throughout its history, ECOSOC has evolved from a forum for general economic discussion into the central mechanism for implementing internationally agreed development goals. The adoption of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development significantly expanded the Council's mandate, placing it at the heart of the effort to achieve the seventeen Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).[^18] Within this framework, ECOSOC provides oversight to a complex network of subsidiary bodies, functional commissions, and specialized agencies. This coordinating role is vital: it ensures that the work of groups like the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) aligns with the broader mission of creating transparent and accountable public institutions.[^15]

In the modern era, ECOSOC has increasingly recognized that financial integrity is a prerequisite for social progress. The Council serves as the critical link between high-level policy and grassroots implementation, particularly regarding SDG 16, which aims to reduce corruption and bribery in all their forms.[^4] By facilitating dialogues between governments, civil society, and the private sector, ECOSOC works to ensure that public funds are not lost to embezzlement but are instead directed toward essential services like education, healthcare, and infrastructure. As the world faces mounting economic disparities, the Council's mission to safeguard the financial resources of nations has never been more urgent.[^24]

## Statement of the Problem

Government embezzlement—the fraudulent appropriation of public funds by those entrusted with their management—represents one of the most significant barriers to global stability and human rights. According to the International Monetary Fund (IMF), bribery and corruption cost the global economy approximately $2 trillion annually, roughly 2% of the world's total GDP.[^5] This financial drain is not merely an economic statistic. It is a direct assault on the welfare of citizens. When public officials siphon funds intended for infrastructure, health, and education into private offshore accounts, they do more than steal money—they steal the future opportunities of their people.[^19]

The problem of embezzlement is intrinsically linked to a lack of transparency. In many jurisdictions, government procurement processes, budget allocations, and state-owned enterprises operate within black boxes, shielded from public oversight and independent audits. This lack of visibility creates an environment where **Grand Corruption** can thrive, allowing billions of dollars to vanish from national treasuries without detection. But the crisis is not limited to high-level theft. **Petty Corruption**—the everyday solicitation of bribes for basic services—acts as a regressive tax on the poor, further widening the gap between the elite and the marginalized.[^6] As global economic conditions tighten, the urgency for ECOSOC to establish a framework for financial accountability has reached a critical threshold.[^15]

### Economic Destabilization

Embezzlement acts as sand in the gears of the global economy, distorting markets and discouraging both domestic and foreign investment. When government contracts are awarded based on kickbacks rather than merit, the quality of public projects suffers, leading to "ghost infrastructure" that exists only on paper or collapses shortly after completion. This inefficiency breeds a climate of uncertainty; investors are hesitant to commit capital to regions where the legal system is perceived as a tool for the powerful rather than a shield for the fair. In 2024, Nasdaq reported that over $3.1 trillion in illicit funds circulated through the global financial system, much of which originated from the theft of public resources.[^17]

The economic impact is particularly devastating for developing nations. While embezzlement is a global crisis, its effects are amplified in economies with limited fiscal buffers. In these contexts, the loss of even a small percentage of the national budget can trigger a sovereign debt crisis. A primary example is the **Tuna Bonds scandal** in Mozambique, where over $2 billion in hidden loans were procured through the bribery of high-ranking officials. When the debt was uncovered, it triggered a collapse of the national currency and a default that pushed an estimated two million people into poverty. This case illustrates that embezzlement is not just a white-collar crime; it is a catalyst for national economic ruin.[^21]

### The Human Deficit: Humanitarian Consequences

Beyond the balance sheets, embezzlement has a profound human cost that directly undermines the United Nations' mission to protect the vulnerable. The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that approximately $455 billion of the $7.35 trillion spent on healthcare globally is lost each year to fraud and corruption. This diversion of resources translates into empty pharmacy shelves, broken medical equipment, and understaffed clinics. Research suggests that in countries with high levels of corruption, child mortality rates are significantly higher because funds meant for immunizations and prenatal care are diverted to private wealth.[^11]

Education suffers a similar fate. When funds for school construction and teacher salaries are embezzled, the result is a generation of children denied the tools for social mobility. This **Human Deficit** creates a cycle of poverty that is nearly impossible to break without systemic reform. ECOSOC must recognize that every dollar stolen from the public purse is a dollar taken from a child's classroom or a patient's hospital bed. In West Africa, for instance, embezzlement within the healthcare sector has even been linked to the proliferation of counterfeit medicines, as state officials take bribes to bypass safety inspections, leading to widespread public health crises.[^12]

### The Transparency Gap: Technological and Legal Failures

The persistence of embezzlement is fueled by what experts call the **Transparency Gap**—a failure to modernize financial oversight in an increasingly digital world. Many governments continue to rely on manual, paper-based accounting systems that are easily manipulated or lost.[^24] Even where digital systems exist, they're often siloed, preventing independent auditors or civil society groups from tracing the flow of money. The U.S. Department of State's 2025 Fiscal Transparency Report found that nearly 50% of the 140 governments assessed failed to meet minimum transparency requirements, often lacking publicly available budget documents or reliable audit reports.[^34]

Furthermore, the rise of virtual assets and decentralized finance (DeFi) has provided corrupt officials with new tools to hide their tracks. In 2025, fines in the cryptocurrency sector for anti-money laundering violations exceeded $1 billion, highlighting how quickly illicit funds can move across borders when oversight is weak.[^14] Without a unified international standard for **Beneficial Ownership**—the legal requirement to disclose who truly owns a company—embezzlers can use shell corporations to buy luxury real estate and assets in foreign countries, effectively laundering their stolen wealth into the legitimate global economy.[^36]

### Erosion of the Social Contract

Perhaps the most dangerous consequence of embezzlement is the erosion of public trust in democratic institutions. When citizens see their leaders enriching themselves at the expense of the public good, the social contract—the unwritten agreement that citizens follow the law in exchange for government protection and services—is broken. This disillusionment often leads to political instability, as marginalized populations lose faith in the possibility of peaceful reform. SDG 16 emphasizes that transparent, effective, and accountable institutions are the foundation of peace. Without them, the vacuum of trust is often filled by civil unrest or extremist movements.[^15]

This political crisis is self-perpetuating. Corruption often leads to the harassment of whistleblowers and journalists who attempt to expose financial crimes. In 2024 alone, over 500 human rights defenders and journalists were killed worldwide, many of whom were investigating the mismanagement of public funds.[^16] When the truth is suppressed and the thieves go unpunished, embezzlement becomes **institutionalized**—meaning it becomes a standard part of how a government operates rather than an exception.[^21] For ECOSOC, the challenge is not just to recover stolen money, but to rebuild the very foundations of trust that allow a society to function.

## History of the Problem

While the misappropriation of public funds is a practice as old as governance itself, the nature of embezzlement has undergone a radical transformation over the last century. In ancient and medieval eras, the distinction between a ruler's personal wealth and the state's treasury was often non-existent, making theft a matter of royal prerogative rather than a legal crime.[^17] But as modern nation-states emerged, so too did the expectation of **fiduciary duty**—the legal obligation of officials to act in the best interest of the public.

The modern crisis of embezzlement is rooted in the mid-20th century, specifically during the post-WWII era of decolonization and the Cold War. As new nations gained independence and international aid began to flow across borders, the lack of robust oversight mechanisms allowed Grand Corruption to take root in many emerging bureaucracies. Over time, the problem evolved from simple cash theft into a sophisticated, globalized system of money laundering, facilitated by the rise of secretive financial jurisdictions.[^30] Today, embezzlement is no longer a localized issue; it is a borderless crime that utilizes the very infrastructure intended to support global trade.

### The Post-Colonial Vacuum

The period following the Second World War saw the birth of dozens of new sovereign states. In many instances, the transition from colonial rule to independence occurred rapidly, leaving behind fragile administrative structures that lacked the checks and balances necessary to protect public funds. During the Cold War, geopolitical interests often overshadowed concerns about financial integrity. Superpowers frequently provided massive amounts of military and economic aid to client states without requiring transparency in how those funds were spent.[^18] This era saw the rise of the **Kleptocrat**—a leader who uses their power to systematically plunder the resources of their country.

A defining example of this era was the regime of Mobutu Sese Seko in Zaire (now the Democratic Republic of the Congo) from 1965 to 1997. Mobutu allegedly embezzled between $4 billion and $15 billion, a sum roughly equivalent to the country's entire external debt at the time of his ousting. This period established a dangerous precedent: as long as a government remained a useful strategic ally, the international community often turned a blind eye to the siphoning of state assets.[^18] This culture of impunity allowed embezzlement to become institutionalized in many regions, creating long-term structural barriers to development that ECOSOC continues to address today.

### The Rise of Tax Havens

In the 1960s and 1970s, the problem of embezzlement shifted from the physical theft of assets to the digital and legal concealment of wealth. This shift was driven by the **Offshore Revolution**, when small jurisdictions emerged—such as the Cayman Islands, the British Virgin Islands, and certain Swiss cantons—that offered absolute bank secrecy and zero taxation.[^20] These **Tax Havens** provided a safe harbor for embezzled funds, allowing corrupt officials to move public money into private accounts that were beyond the reach of national investigators.

By the 1980s, the use of shell companies—entities that exist only on paper—became the standard tool for Grand Corruption. These companies allow individuals to hide their identity as the **Beneficial Owner** of an asset.[^21] For example, a corrupt minister could award a multi-million dollar government contract to a company that they secretly owned through a series of offshore layers. This era marked the point where embezzlement became truly globalized; the money was stolen in one country, laundered in a second, and eventually spent on luxury real estate in a third.[^29] This fragmentation of the crime made it nearly impossible for any single nation to prosecute embezzlers without extensive international cooperation.

### The Era of Leaks: Panama Papers and Beyond

The 21st century has been defined by a series of massive financial scandals that have exposed the staggering scale of modern embezzlement. The most prominent is the **1Malaysia Development Berhad (1MDB) scandal**, where at least $4.50 billion was diverted from a state-run development fund into private accounts—funding luxury lifestyles, Hollywood films, and high-end art.[^22] The 1MDB case was a watershed moment because it demonstrated how high-ranking officials could exploit the global financial system to embezzle funds on a scale previously thought impossible. It also highlighted the complicity of major international banks and audit firms that failed to flag suspicious transactions.

Furthermore, the leak era—beginning with the 2016 Panama Papers and followed by the Paradise and Pandora Papers—provided the world with an unprecedented look into the shadow economy. These leaks consisted of millions of documents from offshore law firms, revealing how current and former world leaders, billionaires, and public officials used secret jurisdictions to hide their wealth.[^23] In many cases, the wealth uncovered in these leaks was directly linked to the embezzlement of public resources or the non-payment of taxes on state-granted monopolies.[^24] These exposures have created a sense of urgency within ECOSOC and other UN bodies, as citizens worldwide have begun to demand higher standards of accountability from their leaders.

## Past Actions

In the decades following the establishment of the United Nations, the international community's approach to embezzlement transitioned from a collection of fragmented national laws to a unified global strategy. Early efforts primarily focused on the demand side of corruption—the prosecution of public officials within their own borders. But as the 1MDB and Tuna Bonds scandals demonstrated, embezzlement is a transnational crime that requires a transnational response.[^16] Today, the framework for action is built upon three pillars: the creation of legally binding treaties, the standardization of financial regulations to prevent money laundering, and the coordination of **Asset Recovery** to return stolen wealth to its rightful owners.

ECOSOC has served as the central hub for these initiatives, often coordinating with the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) to provide technical assistance to Member States. While progress has been made, the international community has often struggled with **Inconsistent Enforcement**, where treaties are signed but not fully integrated into domestic legal systems.[^26] To understand the tools available to delegates today, it is essential to review the key conventions and task forces that have shaped the modern anti-embezzlement landscape.

### The UN Convention against Corruption (UNCAC)

The most comprehensive step taken by the international community is the **United Nations Convention against Corruption (UNCAC)**, adopted by the General Assembly in 2003. UNCAC is the only legally binding universal anti-corruption instrument, with 191 parties currently committed to its mandates.[^11] The Convention is unique because it specifically addresses the embezzlement of public funds in Chapter III, requiring Member States to criminalize the diversion of state property by public officials for their own benefit or for the benefit of another entity.

A breakthrough provision of UNCAC is Chapter V, which establishes Asset Recovery as a fundamental principle of international law.[^4] Prior to this, returning stolen money stashed in foreign banks was a diplomatic nightmare, often taking decades of litigation. UNCAC mandates that requested states (where the money is hidden) must return embezzled public funds to the requesting state (where the money was stolen) once a final judgment is reached. To support this, ECOSOC and the World Bank launched the **Stolen Asset Recovery (StAR) Initiative** in 2007, which has helped countries like Nigeria and the Philippines recover hundreds of millions of dollars in plundered wealth.[^30] The UNCAC Review Mechanism, however, has found that many nations still lack the **Financial Intelligence Units (FIUs)** necessary to track these assets effectively.[^8]

### The OECD Anti-Bribery Convention

While UNCAC focuses on the theft itself, the **Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) Anti-Bribery Convention**, which entered into force in 1999, targets the supply side of embezzlement.[^31] In many cases, public funds are embezzled through kickback schemes, where a private company bribes a government official to overcharge the state for a contract. Before the OECD Convention, many developed nations actually allowed their companies to claim these foreign bribes as tax-deductible business expenses.[^32]

The OECD Convention changed this by requiring signatories to criminalize the act of bribing a foreign public official. This created a level playing field for global trade, ensuring that companies from wealthy nations could be prosecuted in their home courts for corrupt acts committed abroad.[^6] ECOSOC has frequently highlighted the importance of the OECD's peer review process, where member nations audit each other's enforcement of anti-bribery laws. Despite this, a 2024 Transparency International report noted that only a handful of countries are actively enforcing these rules, leading to a **Deterrence Deficit** where companies feel the risk of getting caught is still lower than the potential profit of a corrupt contract.[^6]

### Key UN Resolutions

The normative framework for transparency has also been shaped by a series of General Assembly and ECOSOC resolutions that emphasize the link between financial integrity and the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). Resolution 51/59, which adopted the International Code of Conduct for Public Officials, was an early attempt to standardize the ethical behavior of civil servants, requiring them to disclose personal assets to prevent conflicts of interest.[^9]

More recently, General Assembly Resolution 79/190 (2024) reaffirmed the urgent need to strengthen international cooperation on asset recovery and the return of proceeds of crime.[^39] This resolution specifically called for member states to improve their Beneficial Ownership transparency, ensuring that the real people behind shell companies are identified. ECOSOC has used these resolutions to push for the digitalization of public procurement, arguing that moving government bidding processes online can reduce the human interaction where Petty Corruption and embezzlement thrive.[^20] While these resolutions are not legally binding like UNCAC, they create political pressure that forces member states to justify their financial practices on the global stage.

## Possible Solutions

### Implementing Blockchain in Public Finance

A cutting-edge technological solution to combat embezzlement is the integration of **blockchain technology** into national treasuries. Traditional digital accounting systems often remain siloed and vulnerable to administrative manipulation, as senior officials may possess the override authority to delete or alter transaction records.[^17] Blockchain, or Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT), solves this trust deficit by creating a permanent, encrypted record of every government expenditure.[^41] In a blockchain-based system, once a transaction is verified by the network, it becomes **immutable**, ensuring that phantom employees or fraudulent contractors cannot be paid without leaving an unerasable trail.[^27]

ECOSOC should facilitate technology transfer initiatives to help Member States pilot blockchain for high-risk areas, such as the distribution of social welfare payments or the management of international aid. By using **Smart Contracts**—self-executing programs that only release funds when certain conditions are met—governments can automate the oversight process, reducing the risk of human interference.[^26] The initial cost of this financial overhaul is high, but the long-term savings from reduced leakage could provide a significant transparency dividend for developing economies. Delegates should consider how to balance the need for cryptographic security with the digital sovereignty of Member States who may be wary of hosting their financial data on decentralized networks.[^29]

### The Rollout of Open Data Portals

While blockchain provides a secure internal record, **Open Data Portals** provide a secondary layer of accountability by empowering the public to monitor government spending. These portals are centralized websites where budget allocations, public contracts, and daily expenditures are published in machine-readable formats.[^22] This "sunlight disinfectant" allows journalists, researchers, and ordinary citizens to identify discrepancies—such as a government ministry spending ten times the market rate for office supplies. In Ukraine, the ProZorro e-procurement system saved the government an estimated $1.9 billion in its first two years by exposing corrupt bidding processes to public scrutiny.[^25]

For Open Data Portals to be effective, ECOSOC must advocate for real-time reporting standards. Delayed data is often useless for preventing embezzlement—funds are usually moved offshore within hours of being stolen. These portals must also be paired with digital literacy programs to ensure that civil society groups have the tools to analyze complex financial datasets.[^45] Delegates should focus on creating a universal transparency template that ensures government data is standardized across borders, allowing for the cross-referencing of international contracts and preventing corrupt officials from using complex cross-border layering to hide their tracks.[^32]

### Reforming the UNCAC Review Mechanism

From a legal perspective, creating a new international financial police force would likely face significant diplomatic stalemates due to concerns over national sovereignty and the extreme financial cost of such an entity. Instead, a more viable solution is to strengthen the existing **UNCAC Review Mechanism**. Currently, the peer-review process is often viewed as non-confrontational, where nations review each other's compliance in a confidential and slow-moving cycle.[^46] ECOSOC should propose a transition to mandatory compliance, where the findings of UNCAC reviews are published openly and tied to specific remediation timelines.[^5]

This reformed mechanism would move beyond simple legal analysis and include on-site financial audits conducted by independent UN-certified experts. If a Member State is found to have systemic vulnerabilities—such as a lack of independent auditors or weak anti-money laundering laws—they would receive a "Red Flag" status, signaling to international lenders and investors that the jurisdiction carries a high risk of embezzlement.[^7] By leveraging the economic influence of the IMF and World Bank to enforce UNCAC standards, ECOSOC can ensure that anti-corruption efforts have real-world teeth without requiring the creation of an expensive and politically sensitive new agency.[^19]

### Global Whistleblower Safeguards

The final pillar of a comprehensive solution is the legal protection of the human element in oversight: the **Whistleblower**. Statistics show that tips from employees are the most effective way to detect embezzlement—yet many potential reporters remain silent due to fear of retaliation.[^8] ECOSOC must advocate for a global framework for reporting protections that requires Member States to pass domestic laws guaranteeing the anonymity, legal immunity, and physical safety of individuals who report the misappropriation of public funds.[^12]

This legal solution should include the establishment of **Independent Ombudsman Offices** that operate outside the control of the executive branch.[^3] These offices would serve as safe harbors where officials can submit evidence of embezzlement without alerting their superiors. Furthermore, delegates should discuss the implementation of financial incentive programs, where whistleblowers receive a small percentage of the funds recovered as a result of their report. This "bounty system" has been highly successful in the United States under the False Claims Act, recovering billions in stolen funds while providing the reporter with the financial security needed to start a new life if they are blacklisted from their industry.[^22] By protecting the sentinel, the international community ensures that no embezzler can operate in total secrecy.

## Potential Blocs

The global fight against government embezzlement does not create a simple binary of corrupt versus clean nations; rather, it splits the international community along lines of economic development, legal philosophy, and the interpretation of national sovereignty. These divisions are largely driven by a nation's role in the global financial ecosystem—whether it is a source of plundered capital, a destination for flight wealth, or an emerging economy struggling to build institutional trust. While all Member States publicly endorse the principles of the UN Convention against Corruption (UNCAC), the urgency of the topic reveals deep-seated disagreements over how much authority the international community should have to peer into a sovereign state's private ledgers.

### The Sovereignty Advocates

The most prominent bloc consists of nations that prioritize **Westphalian Sovereignty**—the principle that a state has exclusive authority over its own territory and domestic affairs. For these nations, international mandates for Open Data Portals or mandatory UN-led financial audits are viewed as intrusions on national sovereignty or security. They argue that financial transparency must be balanced against the need for state secrecy in sensitive sectors, such as defense and national infrastructure. This bloc typically resists mandatory remediation timelines and prefers a voluntary, non-binding approach to the UNCAC review process, emphasizing that anti-corruption efforts must be nationally led and context-specific.[^36]

### The Financial Reformers and Asset Recovery Seekers

This bloc is primarily composed of nations that have historically been the victims of Grand Corruption and are now seeking to rebuild their economies. Their primary goal is the unconditional return of stolen assets. These nations often feel that the current international legal framework is unfairly weighted in favor of wealthy requested states, placing an impossible burden of proof on the victimized country to track and identify laundered funds. They advocate for a presumption of illicit origin for the unexplained wealth of public officials and push for stronger international mandates that force foreign banks to cooperate with asset recovery requests without years of litigation.[^40]

### The Regulatory Middle Class

A third group represents nations with established, stable financial sectors that often serve as hubs for international trade. These nations occupy a difficult middle ground; they support global transparency to protect the integrity of their markets, but they are wary of over-regulation that could drive away legitimate investment. Their focus is often on technical standardization, such as the FATF's recommendations and Beneficial Ownership Registers. They generally support transparency but emphasize legal due process, insisting that assets should only be frozen or returned following rigorous judicial review to protect the rights of account holders and prevent the weaponization of anti-corruption laws for political purposes.[^38]

### The Technological Vanguard

Finally, a growing bloc of digital reformers argues that traditional legal frameworks are no longer sufficient in the era of machine-speed finance. This group pushes for the most radical technical solutions, including the mandatory implementation of blockchain ledgers and the use of artificial intelligence to monitor suspicious transaction patterns. They believe that transparency should be automated and absolute, arguing that human-led oversight is inherently prone to bias and bribery. This bloc often clashes with the Sovereignty Advocates over the extraterritorial reach of digital oversight tools, as their proposed solutions often require data sharing that crosses traditional national borders.[^26]

## Glossary

- **Asset Recovery** – The legal process of identifying, freezing, and returning public funds that have been stolen or misappropriated by corrupt officials and stashed in foreign jurisdictions.

- **Automatic Exchange of Information (AEOI)** – A standard that requires financial institutions to report the tax information of non-residents to their local tax authority, which then automatically shares that data with the account holder's home country.

- **Beneficial Ownership** – The identification of the natural person who ultimately owns or controls a legal entity (like a company or trust), even if the entity is registered under a different name.

- **Blockchain** – A decentralized, distributed ledger technology that records transactions across many computers so that the record cannot be altered retroactively without the alteration of all subsequent blocks.

- **Customer Due Diligence (CDD)** – A process used by financial institutions to verify the identity of their clients and assess the risks of illegal activities like money laundering or embezzlement.

- **Distributed Ledger** – A database that is shared and synchronized across multiple sites, institutions, or geographies, accessible by multiple people, and designed to prevent a single authority from controlling the data.

- **E-Governance** – The use of information and communication technologies by government agencies to transform relations with citizens, businesses, and other arms of government to improve service delivery and transparency.

- **Fiduciary Duty** – The legal and ethical obligation of an individual or organization to act in the best interest of another party; in governance, this refers to the duty of officials to manage public funds for the public good.

- **Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU)** – A national agency responsible for receiving, analyzing, and disseminating information regarding suspicious financial transactions to combat money laundering and corruption.

- **Grand Corruption** – Acts committed at a high level of government that distort policies or the central functioning of the state, enabling leaders to benefit at the expense of the public good.

- **Immutable** – A characteristic of data, particularly in blockchain, that means it is unchangeable; once a record has been written to the ledger, it cannot be edited or deleted.

- **Kickback** – A form of negotiated bribery in which a commission is paid to a bribe-taker in exchange for services rendered, often by overcharging the government for a contract.

- **Kleptocrat** – A political leader who uses their power to exploit their people and natural resources of their own territory in order to extend their personal wealth and political power.

- **Machine-Speed Finance** – A term referring to the modern global financial system where capital can be moved across borders in milliseconds through digital networks, often outpacing the speed of legal oversight.

- **Money Laundering** – The process of concealing the origins of money obtained illegally by passing it through a complex sequence of banking transfers or commercial transactions.

- **Offshore Jurisdiction** – A country or territory that provides financial services to non-residents, often characterized by low or no taxes and high levels of banking secrecy (colloquially known as a "tax haven").

- **Open Data** – Data that can be freely used, re-used, and redistributed by anyone—subject only, at most, to the requirement to attribute and share-alike.

- **Petty Corruption** – Everyday abuse of entrusted power by low- and mid-level public officials in their interactions with ordinary citizens, who often are trying to access basic goods or services.

- **Politically Exposed Person (PEP)** – An individual who is or has been entrusted with a prominent public function, such as a head of state or senior politician, and is therefore at higher risk for potential involvement in bribery and corruption.

- **Public Procurement** – The process by which public authorities, such as government departments or local authorities, purchase work, goods, or services from companies.

- **Shell Company** – An inactive company used as a vehicle for various financial maneuvers or to keep control over another company; they often exist only on paper and have no active business operations or significant assets.

- **Smart Contract** – A self-executing contract with the terms of the agreement between buyer and seller being directly written into lines of code, which automatically triggers the release of funds when specific conditions are met.

- **Suspicious Activity Report (SAR)** – A document that financial institutions must file with authorities when they suspect a transaction may be related to illegal activity, such as embezzlement or money laundering.

- **Transparency Gap** – The disparity between the volume of financial data generated by modern economies and the limited capacity of governments or the public to oversee and verify that data.

- **UNCAC Review Mechanism** – The peer-review process where Member States evaluate each other's implementation of the UN Convention against Corruption to identify gaps in national laws and enforcement.

- **Westphalian Sovereignty** – The principle of international law that each nation-state has exclusive sovereignty over its territory and domestic affairs, excluding all external powers.

- **Whistleblower** – A person, usually an employee, who exposes information or activity within a private or public organization that is deemed illegal, unethical, or not correct.
