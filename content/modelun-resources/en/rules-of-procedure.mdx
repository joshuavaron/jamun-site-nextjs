These Rules of Procedure govern all JAMUN committee sessions. Understanding these rules helps you participate effectively in debate, make appropriate motions, and contribute to productive committee sessions. While the rules may seem complex at first, you'll quickly become familiar with them through practice.

## Article I. General Rules

### Rule 1.1 Scope

These rules apply to all JAMUN committees unless otherwise specified by the Secretariat.

### Rule 1.2 Purpose

These Rules of Procedure are designed to facilitate orderly and productive debate, enabling delegates to engage in diplomacy, negotiation, and collaborative problem-solving in the spirit of the United Nations.

### Rule 1.3 Authority of the Chair

**(a)** The Chair shall maintain order, rule on points and motions, and exercise discretion on all procedural matters.

**(b)** The Chair's rulings are final unless overturned by a two-thirds majority vote through a Motion to Appeal the Chair.

**(c)** The Chair may adapt these rules as necessary to facilitate productive debate while maintaining fairness.

### Rule 1.4 Secretariat Interpretation

**(a)** The Secretariat holds final authority to interpret these rules.

**(b)** Any disputes regarding rule interpretation shall be referred to the Secretariat, whose decisions are binding.

### Rule 1.5 Amendments to Rules

**(a)** These rules may be suspended or amended during a session by a two-thirds majority vote.

**(b)** Any suspension or amendment is subject to Secretariat approval.

## Article II. Debate Format

### Rule 2.1 Roll Call

**(a)** Each committee session begins with roll call, during which delegates indicate their presence.

**(b)** When your country is called, respond with one of the following:

**(1)** **"Present"**: You may abstain on substantive votes.

**(2)** **"Present and Voting"**: You must vote yes or no on substantive votes; you may not abstain.

**(c)** Delegates who arrive late should inform the dais in writing.

### Rule 2.2 Speakers' List

**(a)** The Chair maintains the speakers' list throughout debate.

**(b)** Delegates wishing to speak shall raise their placards to be added to the list.

**(c)** A delegate may speak only when recognized by the Chair.

**(d)** Default speaking time is **90 seconds** unless modified by the committee.

**(e)** Debate may not proceed if the speakers' list is empty. If this occurs, the Chair will either open the speakers' list or entertain motions.

### Rule 2.3 Moderated Caucus

**(a)** A moderated caucus is a focused discussion on a specific sub-topic.

**(b)** To propose a moderated caucus, a delegate must specify:

**(1)** Total caucus time (e.g., "10 minutes")

**(2)** Individual speaking time (maximum **one minute**)

**(3)** The specific topic of discussion

**(c)** The motion requires a simple majority to pass.

**(d)** During a moderated caucus:

**(1)** Delegates who wish to speak raise their placards.

**(2)** The Chair calls on delegates in the order they are recognized.

**(3)** Delegates may speak multiple times if time permits.

**(e)** The delegate who proposed the caucus may request the first or last speaking position.

### Rule 2.4 Unmoderated Caucus

**(a)** An unmoderated caucus is informal time for negotiation and coalition-building.

**(b)** To propose an unmoderated caucus, a delegate must specify the total time requested.

**(c)** Maximum duration is **20 minutes**.

**(d)** The motion requires a simple majority to pass.

**(e)** During an unmoderated caucus:

**(1)** Delegates may leave their seats and move freely around the room.

**(2)** Delegates may negotiate informally with other delegates.

**(3)** Delegates typically work on drafting working papers and resolutions.

### Rule 2.5 Yielding Time

**(a)** At the conclusion of a speech from the speakers' list, a delegate may yield remaining time in one of the following ways:

**(1)** **Yield to the Chair**: The speaker's remarks conclude and the Chair moves to the next speaker.

**(2)** **Yield to another delegate**: The named delegate may use the remaining time to speak.

**(b)** Time yielded to another delegate may not be yielded again.

**(c)** Yielding does not apply during moderated caucuses.

## Article III. Points

Points allow delegates to communicate with the Chair about procedural matters, personal needs, or questions. Understanding when and how to use each type of point is essential for effective participation.

### Rule 3.1 Point of Order

**(a)** A Point of Order addresses a procedural violation or error.

**(b)** This point may be raised at any time, including while another delegate is speaking.

**(c)** Delegates should use this point only for genuine procedural concerns, not to interrupt debate.

**(d)** The Chair will rule on the point immediately.

### Rule 3.2 Point of Inquiry

**(a)** A Point of Inquiry is a question directed to the Chair about rules or procedure.

**(b)** This point may be raised only when there is no speaker on the floor.

**(c)** Examples include asking for clarification about a motion or requesting an explanation of voting procedure.

### Rule 3.3 Point of Personal Privilege

**(a)** A Point of Personal Privilege addresses a delegate's personal comfort or ability to participate.

**(b)** This point may be raised when there is no speaker on the floor.

**(c)** Examples include:

**(1)** Unable to hear the speaker

**(2)** Room temperature concerns

**(3)** Need to briefly step out

**(d)** This point should not be used to comment on debate or make substantive remarks.

### Rule 3.4 Point of Information

**(a)** A Point of Information is a question directed to the current speaker, not to the Chair.

**(b)** This point may only be raised during a speech, with the consent of both the Chair and the speaker.

**(c)** When recognized, the delegate asking the question should pose a brief, genuine question—not make a statement disguised as a question.

**(d)** The speaker may decline to answer.

### Rule 3.5 Precedence of Points

Points are considered in the following order of precedence:

**(1)** Point of Order (highest—may interrupt speakers)

**(2)** Point of Inquiry

**(3)** Point of Personal Privilege

**(4)** Point of Information

## Article IV. Motions

Motions allow delegates to change what the committee is doing. Understanding the different motions and their proper use is key to effective participation.

### Rule 4.1 General Motion Rules

**(a)** Motions must be raised when there is no speaker on the floor.

**(b)** The Chair will recognize motions by asking delegates to raise their placards.

**(c)** Motions require a second from another delegate before being voted on.

**(d)** The Chair may rule any motion dilatory (obstructing progress) and choose not to entertain it.

### Rule 4.2 Order of Precedence

When multiple motions are on the floor, they are considered in order from most to least disruptive:

**(1)** Motion to Appeal the Chair

**(2)** Motion to Adjourn

**(3)** Motion to Suspend

**(4)** Motion to Enter Voting Procedure

**(5)** Motion to Close Debate

**(6)** Motion to Open Debate

**(7)** Motion to Open the Speakers' List

**(8)** Motion for Unmoderated Caucus

**(9)** Motion for Moderated Caucus

Within the same type of motion, longer durations are considered more disruptive and are voted on first.

### Rule 4.3 Motion to Appeal the Chair

**(a)** This motion challenges a ruling made by the Chair.

**(b)** This motion cannot be ruled dilatory.

**(c)** Requires a two-thirds majority to pass.

**(d)** If passed, the Chair's ruling is overturned.

### Rule 4.4 Motion to Adjourn

**(a)** This motion ends the conference session entirely.

**(b)** Requires a simple majority to pass.

**(c)** This motion is rarely used except at the end of the final committee session.

### Rule 4.5 Motion to Suspend

**(a)** This motion suspends the committee session for recess.

**(b)** Requires a simple majority to pass.

**(c)** Typically used for lunch breaks or at the end of a session day.

### Rule 4.6 Motion to Enter Voting Procedure

**(a)** This motion moves the committee into voting on draft resolutions.

**(b)** Requires a simple majority to pass.

**(c)** Once in voting procedure, no further debate occurs and all draft resolutions are voted on.

**(d)** Delegates should ensure they are ready to vote before making this motion.

### Rule 4.7 Motion to Close Debate

**(a)** This motion ends debate on the current topic.

**(b)** Requires a two-thirds majority to pass.

**(c)** If passed, the committee will move toward voting on any draft resolutions on the floor.

**(d)** This motion should only be made when substantial debate has occurred.

### Rule 4.8 Motion to Open Debate

**(a)** This motion opens debate on a new topic (in committees with multiple topics).

**(b)** Requires a simple majority to pass.

### Rule 4.9 Motion to Open the Speakers' List

**(a)** This motion opens or reopens the speakers' list.

**(b)** Requires a simple majority to pass.

**(c)** Use this motion when the speakers' list has been exhausted or not yet opened.

### Rule 4.10 Motion for Unmoderated Caucus

**(a)** This motion requests informal negotiation time.

**(b)** Must specify total time (maximum 20 minutes).

**(c)** Requires a simple majority to pass.

### Rule 4.11 Motion for Moderated Caucus

**(a)** This motion requests focused discussion on a specific sub-topic.

**(b)** Must specify total time, individual speaking time (maximum 1 minute), and topic.

**(c)** Requires a simple majority to pass.

## Article V. Working Papers and Resolutions

### Rule 5.1 Working Papers

**(a)** Working papers are informal documents used to develop and share ideas.

**(b)** Working papers do not require sponsors or signatories.

**(c)** Introduction of working papers to the committee is at the discretion of the Chair.

**(d)** Working papers are not voted on—they are stepping stones toward draft resolutions.

### Rule 5.2 Draft Resolutions

**(a)** Draft resolutions are formal documents proposing solutions to the committee's topic.

**(b)** Draft resolutions require:

**(1)** **Sponsors** (main authors): Maximum of **5% of delegates in committee, rounded up**.

**(2)** **Signatories** (supporters who want it debated): Minimum of **20% of present delegates, rounded down**.

**(c)** Draft resolutions must follow proper format:

**(1)** Header with committee name, topic, sponsors, and signatories

**(2)** Preambulatory clauses (background and context)

**(3)** Operative clauses (proposed actions)

**(d)** Being a signatory does not obligate a delegate to vote for the resolution.

### Rule 5.3 Amendments

**(a)** Amendments propose changes to a draft resolution.

**(b)** **Friendly amendments**:

**(1)** Approved by all sponsors of the draft resolution.

**(2)** Automatically incorporated without a vote.

**(c)** **Unfriendly amendments**:

**(1)** Not approved by all sponsors.

**(2)** Require their own sponsors and signatories.

**(3)** Must be voted on by the committee.

**(4)** Require a simple majority to pass.

## Article VI. Voting Procedure

### Rule 6.1 Procedural Votes

**(a)** Procedural votes are votes on motions (such as motions for caucuses or to close debate).

**(b)** All delegates must vote on procedural matters; abstentions are not permitted.

**(c)** A simple majority (more than half of votes cast) is required for a procedural vote to pass.

**(d)** Procedural votes are conducted by a show of placards.

### Rule 6.2 Substantive Votes

**(a)** Substantive votes are votes on draft resolutions and amendments.

**(b)** Voting options depend on your roll call response:

**(1)** Delegates who responded "Present" may vote Yes, No, or Abstain.

**(2)** Delegates who responded "Present and Voting" must vote Yes or No; they may not abstain.

**(c)** A simple majority of non-abstaining votes is required for a substantive vote to pass.

### Rule 6.3 Roll Call Vote

**(a)** Any delegate may request a roll call vote before voting begins.

**(b)** During a roll call vote, each country is called individually.

**(c)** Voting options are:

**(1)** **"Yes"**: Vote in favor

**(2)** **"No"**: Vote against

**(3)** **"Abstain"**: Neither for nor against (only if "Present" at roll call)

**(4)** **"Pass"**: Defer your vote until the end of the roll call

**(d)** A delegate who votes "Pass" will have one opportunity to cast their vote (Yes or No only) at the end of the roll call.

### Rule 6.4 Voting Procedure Rules

**(a)** Once voting procedure begins, no delegates may enter or leave the committee room.

**(b)** No debate or discussion occurs during voting procedure.

**(c)** Delegates should remain silent except when casting their votes.

## Article VII. Delegate Conduct

### Rule 7.1 Diplomatic Courtesy

**(a)** Delegates shall maintain diplomatic courtesy at all times.

**(b)** Personal attacks on other delegates are strictly prohibited.

**(c)** Delegates should address others through the Chair (e.g., "The delegate would like to ask the delegate of France...").

**(d)** Delegates should refer to themselves in the third person as "the delegation of [Country]."

### Rule 7.2 Electronic Devices

**(a)** Use of electronic devices during committee sessions is at the discretion of the Chair.

**(b)** Devices should be used only for research, note-taking, and resolution writing.

**(c)** Delegates should not use devices for personal communication or entertainment during sessions.

### Rule 7.3 Dress Code

**(a)** Delegates are expected to wear formal attire during committee sessions.

**(b)** JAMUN staff understand that formal attire varies across cultures and personal circumstances.

**(c)** Acceptable attire includes suits, dresses, dress shirts, slacks, blouses, and blazers.

**(d)** Delegates are politely asked not to wear clothing with distracting logos, messages, or images.

### Rule 7.4 Violations and Consequences

**(a)** For a first offense, the delegate will receive a verbal warning from the Chair.

**(b)** For a second offense, a written note will be sent to the delegate's faculty advisor.

**(c)** For a third offense, the delegate may be removed from committee at the Chair's discretion.

**(d)** Serious misconduct may result in immediate escalation regardless of prior warnings.
